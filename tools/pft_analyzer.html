The Basics

What Are PFTs?

Pulmonary function tests (PFTs) are a battery of non-invasive tests that measure how well the lungs move air (spirometry), how much air they hold (lung volumes), and how efficiently they transfer gas across the alveolar-capillary membrane (DLCO). Together they characterize lung physiology in a way no imaging study can.

Per the 2022 ATS/ERS Interpretive Strategy, the goal of interpretation is to identify the pattern of abnormality, its severity, and to guide clinical reasoning — not to diagnose diseases directly.

Why the Lower Limit of Normal (LLN) Matters

The old approach of calling anything <80% predicted "abnormal" is no longer recommended. The ATS/ERS 2022 guidelines mandate using the 5th percentile (LLN) derived from reference equations (GLI-2012) as the threshold for abnormality.

⚠ Using 80% predicted creates a fixed cutoff that misclassifies young, tall individuals (over-diagnosis) and older, shorter individuals (under-diagnosis). LLN accounts for age, sex, height, and ethnicity.

The GLI-2012 equations now include race-neutral and race-specific options. ATS 2023 statement encourages clinicians to be aware of the potential for race-based equations to introduce bias.

The Z-Score Framework

A Z-score expresses how many standard deviations a value falls from the predicted mean. The LLN corresponds to Z = −1.645 (5th percentile). Z-scores allow comparison across different parameters and track longitudinal change.

Quality, Acceptability & Picking the Best Maneuver

Before interpreting anything — you must know if you can trust the numbers · ATS/ERS 2019 Technical Standards

The Big Picture: What Happens During Spirometry

The patient performs up to 8 forced expiratory maneuvers. Each one generates a flow-volume loop and a volume-time curve simultaneously. Your job before any interpretation: (1) decide which maneuvers are acceptable, and (2) from acceptable maneuvers, check reproducibility. Only then do you extract values.

💡 You report the highest FEV₁ and highest FVC from any acceptable maneuver — they do not have to come from the same maneuver. The FEV₁/FVC ratio is then calculated from the maneuver with the best combined FEV₁ + FVC.

Step 1 — Acceptability Criteria (Each Maneuver)

Each maneuver must pass ALL of the following to be considered acceptable:

A · Good Start — Back-Extrapolation Volume (BEV)

The patient must blast air out from the very first millisecond. In reality, there is always a tiny ramp-up. Back-extrapolation projects the steepest part of the volume-time curve back to find the true time zero — the theoretical instant of maximal flow onset.

BEV must be <5% of FVC OR <150 mL (whichever is greater).

If BEV is too large → patient hesitated before blasting → FEV₁ is overestimated (you're counting volume exhaled before true time zero). On the VTC this looks like a slow rounded start rather than a sharp vertical rise.

Coaching fix: "The moment you seal your lips around the mouthpiece — blast. No hesitation. Like you're blowing out birthday candles as fast as you can."

B · No Cough in the First Second

A cough during the first second creates a pressure transient that invalidates FEV₁ — it's no longer a pure measure of airflow limitation. A cough after the first second is acceptable but should be noted. On the VTC: visible as a sharp spike or step. On the FVL: notch in the expiratory limb.

⚠ Cough in the first second = unacceptable maneuver, full stop. Do not extract FEV₁ from it.

C · Satisfactory Exhalation — No Early Termination

The patient must exhale until a true plateau is reached OR for a minimum duration:

  • Plateau criterion: Volume change <25 mL over any 1-second period → true end of exhalation reached (RV achieved)
  • Duration minimum: ≥6 seconds for adults; ≥3 seconds for children under 10
  • In severe obstruction: May need 15+ seconds — allow it. Terminating early in COPD/emphysema underestimates FVC and overestimates the degree of obstruction
  • Early termination sign: On VTC, the curve stops abruptly without a flat plateau. On FVL, the expiratory limb ends abruptly before reaching the x-axis

D · No Other Artifacts

  • Glottic closure (Valsalva): Patient inadvertently closes glottis mid-exhalation → sudden stop in flow → abrupt notch on FVL. Common in elderly patients.
  • Variable effort: Patient eases up mid-maneuver → flow drops prematurely → falsely scooped curve that mimics obstruction
  • Leak around mouthpiece: Volume lost → FVC underestimated. Check lip seal throughout
  • Tongue obstructing mouthpiece: Blunted PEF that is inconsistent across maneuvers

Need a minimum of 3 acceptable maneuvers. From those, the two largest FVC values and the two largest FEV₁ values must each be within:

What Is TGV (Thoracic Gas Volume)?

TGV — The Plethysmography Equivalent of FRC

TGV (Thoracic Gas Volume) is measured by body plethysmography and represents the volume of gas in the thorax at the point of airway occlusion — which is typically at FRC (end-tidal expiration). It is the plethysmographic measurement of FRC.

How it's measured: Patient sits in the sealed plethysmograph box and breathes tidally. At end-expiration, a shutter occludes the airway. The patient then performs gentle panting efforts against the closed shutter. By measuring the changes in mouth pressure (≈ alveolar pressure) and box volume (≈ change in thoracic volume) simultaneously, Boyle's Law gives us the thoracic gas volume:

P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ → TGV = ΔV_box × P_alveolar / ΔP_mouth
⚠ Key distinction: TGV measures ALL intrathoracic gas — including gas trapped behind closed airways (bullae, severely obstructed lung units). Gas dilution techniques (He dilution, N₂ washout) only measure communicating gas. In severe emphysema, TGV >> He-dilution FRC — the difference = trapped gas volume.
⚠ Panting frequency artifact: If the patient pants too fast (>2 Hz) in severe obstruction, pressure equilibration across the obstructed airways is incomplete → TGV is overestimated. Coach slow panting (1 Hz). This is why some labs report implausibly enormous TGV in severe COPD.

SVC vs FVC — A Quality Check Too

After performing spirometry, compare FVC to SVC (slow vital capacity). This is both a quality check and a physiologic clue:

  • SVC ≈ FVC (within 150–200 mL): No significant air trapping during forced exhalation. FVC is reliable.
  • SVC > FVC by >200 mL: Dynamic airway collapse during forced exhalation — the effort itself causes early airway closure and traps gas. FVC underestimates true VC. Use SVC for reporting VC and for calculating TLC from plethysmography.
  • SVC > FVC by >500 mL: Significant dynamic collapse — severe COPD or emphysema. This also means the "low FVC" you see may be falsely suggesting restriction — it's not restriction, it's air trapping.
✓ Always report the larger of SVC and FVC as the vital capacity for TLC calculations and pattern interpretation.

ATS Acceptability Criteria (2019)

For each maneuver to be acceptable:

  • Good start: Back-extrapolated volume <5% of FVC or <150 mL (whichever is greater)
  • No cough during the first second of exhalation
  • No early termination: Plateau on volume-time curve (change <25 mL in 1 sec) OR ≥15 sec exhalation (or age ≥10 forced to complete)

Bronchodilator Response (BDR)

Per ATS/ERS 2022, a positive BDR is defined as an increase in FEV₁ or FVC of ≥10% of predicted (not 12% of baseline as previously used). This change was made to normalize for baseline severity.

✓ New 2022 threshold: ≥10% predicted AND ≥200 mL absolute increase in FEV₁ or FVC → Positive BDR

A positive BDR suggests reversible airflow limitation (e.g., asthma), but is not diagnostic. GOLD does not require reversibility for COPD diagnosis.

⚠ BDR does not predict long-term response to bronchodilators. About 50% of COPD patients show BDR at some point.

Measurement Methods

Body Plethysmography
  • Gold standard — measures total thoracic gas volume (TGV ≈ FRC)
  • Measures all gas, including trapped/non-communicating gas
  • Preferred in COPD, emphysema, bullous disease
  • Requires patient cooperation with panting maneuver against closed shutter
  • Can overestimate FRC in severe obstruction if panting frequency too fast
Gas Dilution (He or N₂ washout)
  • Measures only communicating gas — will underestimate in air trapping
  • He dilution: rebreath closed circuit with known [He] until equilibration
  • N₂ washout: breathe 100% O₂ until [N₂] <1.5%
  • Useful if plethysmograph unavailable; acceptable for non-obstructive disease
  • Pleth − Gas dilution FRC difference = trapped gas volume

DLCO

Diffusing Capacity for Carbon Monoxide · ATS/ERS 2017 Technical Standard

What DLCO Measures

DLCO (also called TLCO in Europe) measures the rate of CO transfer from alveolar gas into pulmonary capillary blood per unit driving pressure. It reflects the integrity of the entire gas exchange unit: alveolar surface area, membrane thickness, capillary blood volume, and hemoglobin.

⚠ ATS reproducibility: 2 acceptable maneuvers within 2 mL/min/mmHg of each other (or within 10%). Wait ≥4 min between maneuvers to allow CO washout.

Causes of Reduced DLCO

↓ Alveolar Surface Area
  • Emphysema — destruction of alveolar walls
  • Surgical resection — pneumonectomy, lobectomy
  • Atelectasis — collapsed lung units
  • Air trapping — uneven distribution of test gas
↓ Membrane Diffusion / Capillary Blood
  • ILD/Fibrosis — thickened alveolar-capillary membrane
  • Pulmonary vascular disease — PAH, CTEPH (reduced capillary recruitment)
  • Anemia — reduced Hb available to bind CO
  • PE — dead space; capillary obstruction
↑ Elevated DLCO — When to Consider
  • Pulmonary hemorrhage (alveolar Hb binding CO) — classic cause; Goodpasture's, DAH
  • Polycythemia — increased circulating Hb
  • Left-to-right shunt — increased pulmonary blood flow
  • Asthma (mild, acute) — increased pulmonary blood flow during bronchoconstriction

Hemoglobin Correction

DLCO is linearly related to hemoglobin. Always correct DLCO for Hb, especially in anemia or polycythemia.

DLCOcorrected = DLCOmeasured × (10.22 + Hb) / (1.7 × Hb) [females]
DLCOcorrected = DLCOmeasured × (10.22 + Hb) / (1.7 × Hb) [males: ref Hb = 14.6 g/dL]
Without Hb correction, anemia falsely lowers DLCO and may lead to erroneous diagnosis of ILD or vascular disease.

Common Pitfalls

  • Obstructive pseudorestriction: In severe obstruction, air trapping causes elevated RV which "steals" from VC → FVC appears low → looks restrictive. TLC is normal or elevated. This is NOT true restriction.
  • FEV₁/FVC ratio artifactually normal: In pure restriction, both FEV₁ and FVC fall proportionally → ratio preserved → pattern may look normal on ratio alone. Always check raw FVC and FEV₁.
  • DLCO without volume correction: Low DLCO due to low VA (e.g., poor effort, small lungs) will be corrected when expressed as KCO. Always report both.
  • Race-based equations: Be aware of ongoing controversy. Applying race-based equations may systematically underestimate disease in non-white patients. Consider reporting with multiple reference sets when relevant.
  • Longitudinal change: Serial PFTs should use consistent reference equations and technologists. A 15% decline in FVC or DLCO over time is clinically significant in ILD monitoring.

The CPFE Paradox

Combined Pulmonary Fibrosis and Emphysema is a pattern (not a diagnosis) where upper lobe emphysema and lower lobe fibrosis coexist. The opposing effects on lung volumes cancel each other out:

Fibrosis pulls volumes down → Emphysema pulls volumes up → Net: normal or near-normal spirometry and TLC. But DLCO is devastatingly low (both pathologies impair gas transfer). PAH is common and worsens prognosis.

FVC vs. SVC — When & Why It Matters

The slow vital capacity is one of the most underused tools in the PFT lab

What Is SVC (Slow Vital Capacity)?

SVC is the maximal volume exhaled slowly and completely from TLC to RV — no forced effort, no time pressure. It should theoretically equal FVC because both measure the same volume (TLC − RV). In healthy lungs, they are nearly identical. In disease, they diverge — and that divergence tells you something important.

💡 Key principle: FVC is an effort-dependent, time-limited maneuver. When airways collapse prematurely during forced exhalation (dynamic airway compression), the patient cannot exhale all their air before RV is reached — they trap gas. SVC, done slowly, avoids this collapse and gives a truer estimate of VC.

The SVC > FVC Gap — What It Means

SVC vs FVCInterpretationMechanismClinical Context
SVC ≈ FVC
Difference <150–200 mL		 No significant air trapping during forced maneuver Airways remain open throughout forced exhalation Normal, mild restriction, or mild disease
SVC > FVC
Difference >200–300 mL		 Dynamic airway collapse — forced exhalation causes early airway closure, trapping gas that SVC can recover High driving pressure during forced effort → airway compression → gas trapped behind closed airways COPD, emphysema, moderate-severe asthma, bronchiectasis
SVC >> FVC
Difference >500 mL		 Significant air trapping / severe dynamic collapse Severely floppy airways (emphysema) or severe obstruction Advanced COPD, emphysema — correlates with RV elevation

Why This Changes Your Interpretation

This is the obstructive pseudorestriction problem in practice. When you only have FVC:

FVC low + FEV₁/FVC normal → "Possible restriction"
↓
Check SVC: Is SVC significantly higher than FVC?
↓
SVC > FVC by >200 mL:
The "low FVC" is from air trapping — not restriction. True VC is better reflected by SVC. Obstruction with air trapping masquerading as restriction.
SVC ≈ FVC:
The reduced FVC is real. Proceed to TLC measurement to confirm restriction.
⚠ In practice, use the largest VC (whether from SVC or FVC) when calculating TLC = VC + RV. Using a falsely low FVC will underestimate TLC and may falsely suggest restriction even on plethysmography.

Which VC to Report?

  • Always report the larger of SVC and FVC as the "vital capacity" — this is the ATS/ERS recommendation
  • When calculating TLC from spirometry + plethysmography, use the largest VC available
  • If SVC and FVC are similar, either is acceptable — FVC is used for primary spirometric interpretation
  • In COPD patients especially, always perform SVC — the gap is clinically meaningful and affects severity grading
  • A patient with FVC = 2.0 L and SVC = 2.6 L has meaningful air trapping — their "restriction" is an artifact

Practical Tips in the PFT Lab

  • SVC technique: Patient breathes to TLC (maximal inspiration), then exhales as completely as possible at a comfortable pace — no blast, no rushing. Typical exhalation time 6–15 sec.
  • ERV maneuver: Can also do SVC from FRC upward (inspiratory VC) — volume should be identical
  • Coach the patient: "Breathe all the way in… now let it all out slowly, all the way until you can't push any more." Effort at the very end of exhalation matters — incomplete effort at RV is the most common error.
  • When FVC <SVC: Document both values and report the gap. Note: "FVC <SVC by X mL, consistent with dynamic airway collapse / air trapping during forced maneuver."
  • Pre- and post-BD SVC: SVC often increases more than FVC after bronchodilator in COPD — reflects reversal of dynamic hyperinflation. This is why some patients feel better after BD even when FEV₁ barely budges.

The Scooping Assessment Framework

Scooping = concavity of the expiratory limb. Here's how to evaluate it systematically:

STEP 1 — Identify the Peak Expiratory Flow (PEF)
In normal lungs, PEF occurs very early (~15–20% of FVC exhaled). It should be sharp and high. A blunted or delayed PEF suggests effort or upper airway issue.
↓
STEP 2 — Assess the descending limb shape
After PEF, draw an imaginary straight line from PEF to the end of the curve (where it meets the x-axis at RV). Is the actual curve above or below this line?
• Above = convex = normal
• Below = concave = scooped = obstruction
↓
STEP 3 — Where does scooping occur?
• Early (first 25% of FVC): large airway disease, tracheal, significant obstruction
• Mid (FEF₂₅₋₇₅ zone): typical COPD, classic scooping
• Late only (last 25%): small airway disease — subtle, may have normal FEV₁/FVC
↓
STEP 4 — Check the terminal portion
Does the curve reach zero flow smoothly? Or does it tail off asymptotically (never quite reaching zero)? An asymptotic tail = severe air trapping — the patient runs out of drive before fully emptying.
↓
STEP 5 — Look at the inspiratory limb
Normal: smooth semicircle. Flattened = variable extrathoracic UAO (inspiration is effort-independent above the glottis). Both limbs flat = fixed UAO.

The Physics of Why Scooping Happens

Equal Pressure Point (EPP) Theory

During forced exhalation, airway pressure decreases as you move from alveolus toward mouth (resistive pressure drop). At some point — the Equal Pressure Point — the airway pressure equals pleural pressure. Downstream from EPP (toward mouth), transmural pressure is negative: the airway is compressed by surrounding pleural pressure.

In healthy lungs: EPP is in the large, cartilaginous airways — they resist compression. Flow is high.

In emphysema/COPD: Loss of elastic recoil → low driving pressure → EPP moves upstream into small, floppy airways → dynamic compression occurs earlier at lower lung volumes → flow falls off steeply → scooping.

This is why scooping worsens as volume decreases (toward RV) — elastic recoil is lower at low volumes, EPP migrates further upstream, and small airways collapse earlier. This is also why flow at 50% and 25% of FVC (FEF₅₀, FEF₂₅) is most sensitive to small airway disease.

Upper Airway Obstruction Patterns

TypeExpiratory LimbInspiratory LimbCause
Fixed UAO Flattened plateau Flattened plateau Tracheal stenosis, subglottic stricture, goiter, tracheal tumor — obstruction is rigid, does not change with breathing phase
Variable Extrathoracic Normal Flattened Vocal cord dysfunction (VCD), bilateral vocal cord paralysis, subglottic malacia — during inspiration, negative pressure collapses floppy extrathoracic airway; during exhalation, positive pressure opens it
Variable Intrathoracic Flattened Normal Tracheomalacia, bronchomalacia — during exhalation, positive pleural pressure collapses floppy intrathoracic trachea; during inspiration, negative pleural pressure opens it
⚠ Quantifying UAO: Empey index = FEV₁(mL) / PEF(L/min). If >10, suggests large airway obstruction. Also: FEF₅₀/FIF₅₀ ratio — normally ~1. >1 = expiratory predominance (intrathoracic or fixed); <1 = inspiratory predominance (extrathoracic).

Subtle Patterns to Know

  • Sawtooth pattern: Oscillations on expiratory or both limbs → upper airway instability, seen in OSA, Parkinson's, REM behavior disorder. Caused by rhythmic partial collapse of pharyngeal walls.
  • Notch on expiratory limb: Sudden dip or step partway through exhalation → glottic closure (patient holds breath momentarily), cough artifact, or variable effort. Usually a technical artifact.
  • High PEF with rapid flow fall: "Ski slope" pattern — very high initial flow that collapses quickly → emphysema with severely reduced recoil. Despite good initial effort, driving pressure is lost quickly.
  • Low PEF with preserved mid-flow: Suggests poor effort or upper airway weakness rather than obstructive lung disease. Check reproducibility and coaching.
  • Inspiratory loop larger than expiratory: Rare — seen in obesity or neuromuscular disease where expiratory muscles are very weak.
  • Normal FVL with abnormal spirometry numbers: Shape is normal but shifted — loop is narrow (restriction) or the tail is prolonged. Always look at both the numbers and the shape.

Reading the Loop Before Looking at Numbers

A Disciplined Approach

Train yourself to look at the loop before reading the printed values. Ask in order:

  • Is the loop wide or narrow? Width = FVC. Narrow → restriction or air trapping.
  • Is the expiratory peak sharp or blunted? Blunted → poor effort or UAO.
  • Is the descending limb straight, convex, or concave? Concave = obstructed.
  • Where does scooping begin? Early = large airway; late = small airway.
  • Does the curve reach the x-axis cleanly? Asymptotic tail = air trapping.
  • Is the inspiratory limb normal? Flat = UAO (extrathoracic or fixed).
  • Are there artifacts? Sawtooth, notches, variable effort, early termination.
✓ If your loop-based gestalt and the numbers disagree — trust your gestalt and look for technical issues with the maneuver. A perfect-looking loop with "abnormal" numbers often means a reference equation mismatch or borderline finding.

Low VA with Normal FVC — What Now?

One of the most common and underappreciated PFT conundrums

The Setup

You get a DLCO result back. FVC is normal (or near-normal). But VA — the alveolar volume measured during the single-breath DLCO maneuver — is low. No plethysmography was ordered. What does this mean? Is the DLCO genuinely low, or is it low because VA is low?

⚠ This is a critical interpretive problem. DLCO depends on the volume of gas distributed during the breath-hold. If the patient didn't inhale fully (low VA), DLCO will be falsely low — not because of disease, but because less lung participated in gas exchange.

What VA Actually Measures

VA is calculated from the helium dilution during the single-breath DLCO maneuver. The patient inspires from RV to TLC — if they do this fully, VA ≈ TLC. If they don't fully inspire (submaximal effort, obesity limiting TLC, true restriction, or gas maldistribution), VA will be lower than true TLC.

Reason VA is LowFVCDLCOKCOAction
Submaximal inspiration (technical) Normal Falsely ↓ Normal or ↑ Repeat with coaching; check inspiratory volume ≥85–90% of VC
True restriction (small TLC) ↓ ↓ (proportional) Normal or ↑ Get TLC by plethysmography
Gas maldistribution (uneven ventilation) Normal or ↓ ↓ Normal or ↓ VA underestimates true lung volume; DLCO interpretation unreliable
Obesity (low FRC, small ERV) Normal or mildly ↓ Mildly ↓ or N Normal or ↑ KCO elevated = clue to obesity effect

The KCO (DLCO/VA) — Your Most Important Tool Here

Using KCO to Disentangle Low VA

KCO = DLCO ÷ VA. It normalizes diffusing capacity for the volume of lung that participated in the test. When VA is low:

VA is low. Is KCO normal, elevated, or low?
↓
KCO Normal or ↑

The lung tissue that did participate is healthy. Low VA is from: (a) submaximal effort — repeat the test, or (b) true restriction — the small amount of lung present is intrinsically normal. Get TLC to confirm.
KCO Low

Even the lung that participated is diseased. This is genuine parenchymal or vascular pathology — emphysema, ILD, PAH. Low DLCO is real, not just a volume artifact.
KCO High

With low VA: strongly suggests restriction — remaining lung is over-recruited (doing the work of the missing volume). Also seen in obesity, polycythemia, alveolar hemorrhage.

The VA/TLC Ratio — When You Do Have TLC

When plethysmographic TLC is available, compare it to VA from the DLCO maneuver:

VA/TLC ratio (normal ≥0.85–0.90)

If VA/TLC <0.85 → significant gas maldistribution — unevenly ventilated regions are not sampled by the He bolus. DLCO is an underestimate of true diffusing capacity because poorly ventilated lung units received no test gas. This is most pronounced in severe emphysema with bullae.
⚠ When VA/TLC is very low (e.g., 0.60), DLCO may severely underestimate true diffusing capacity. Some labs will note: "DLCO may underestimate true diffusing capacity due to significant gas maldistribution (VA/TLC = X)."

What To Do When You Have Normal FVC but Low VA and No TLC

Decision Algorithm
FVC normal + VA low on DLCO + No TLC available
↓
Step 1: Check inspiratory volume (IV) for the DLCO maneuver
IV should be ≥85% of the largest VC. If IV <85% of VC → submaximal inspiration → technically inadequate test → repeat with coaching.
↓
Step 2: If IV was adequate, check KCO
KCO normal or elevated → low VA likely reflects true small lungs or obesity. Report: "DLCO reduced; KCO preserved, suggesting volume-dependent reduction rather than intrinsic membrane pathology."
↓
Step 3: Compare VA to FVC
VA should approximate TLC, which is always >FVC. If VA <FVC → definitely submaximal inspiration — the patient didn't even inspire to their FVC volume. This test is invalid for DLCO.
↓
Step 4: Recommend plethysmography
This is the definitive answer. Note in your report: "TLC measurement recommended to clarify whether low VA reflects true restriction vs. gas maldistribution vs. submaximal effort."
✓ The critical rule: If VA < FVC, the DLCO test is technically invalid. The patient did not inspire fully. No interpretation should be made from DLCO in this setting. Repeat the test.

Putting It Together — Worked Example

FVC = 3.2 L (92% pred)  |  FEV₁/FVC = 0.80  |  FEV₁ = 88% pred
DLCO = 52% pred  |  VA = 3.8 L  |  KCO = 68% pred
TLC by plethysmography: not done
Analysis:
• Spirometry: normal (no obstruction, FVC normal)
• VA = 3.8 L vs FVC = 3.2 L → VA > FVC ✓ (adequate inspiration — reached at least above FVC)
• However, TLC is typically FVC + RV. RV ~1.2–1.5 L → expected TLC ~4.4–4.7 L. VA = 3.8 L is low for expected TLC → some gas maldistribution or mildly submaximal effort.
• KCO = 68% predicted → below LLN → even the lung that participated is diseased.
• Interpretation: Moderately reduced DLCO with reduced KCO, in the setting of normal spirometry. Pattern consistent with pulmonary vascular disease (PAH, CTEPH) or early ILD. CPFE cannot be excluded. Recommend TLC + CT chest.

Caveats, Pitfalls & Edge Cases

The things that trip everyone up · ERV · VA alone · Pseudo-restriction · Patterns that lie

Can I Use VA Alone to Say Restriction Without TLC?

Short answer: No. But VA gives you useful clues.

VA is measured during the single-breath DLCO maneuver via helium dilution. It approximates TLC only if the patient inhaled fully (inspiration ≥85% of VC) and gas distribution is uniform. VA is almost always ≤ TLC — never greater. So:

VA is low. FVC is normal or also low. No TLC available.
↓
First: Was inspiration adequate? Check inspiratory volume (IV) for the DLCO maneuver. IV must be ≥85% of the largest VC. If VA < FVC → the patient didn't even inspire to their own FVC → test is technically invalid. Repeat.
↓
If IV was adequate: Check KCO (DLCO/VA). If KCO is normal or elevated → the lung tissue present is healthy. Low VA + normal KCO = small lungs (restriction) OR obesity (high KCO). Low VA + low KCO = diseased lung tissue (emphysema, ILD, vascular disease).
↓
Bottom line: VA alone cannot confirm restriction. You can say "VA is reduced, consistent with restriction, but TLC measurement is needed to confirm." Never diagnose restriction from VA alone in a report.
⚠ VA/TLC ratio (when TLC available): if <0.85, significant gas maldistribution — poorly ventilated units not sampled by He bolus → DLCO underestimates true diffusing capacity. Common in emphysema with bullae.

Can I Rely on ERV?

ERV (Expiratory Reserve Volume) — Use With Extreme Caution

ERV = FRC − RV = the volume you can exhale below tidal end-expiration. It sounds useful but is the most effort-dependent and variable of all lung volume compartments. Problems:

  • Highly variable within sessions: ERV can change 20–30% between maneuvers depending on where the patient is breathing tidally. FRC itself varies breath to breath.
  • Obesity: ERV is dramatically reduced in obesity because abdominal contents push the diaphragm up, reducing FRC. An obese patient may have ERV of 0.3–0.5 L vs. 1.0–1.5 L expected. This does NOT mean lung disease.
  • Supine position: ERV falls significantly when supine — if testing is done semi-reclined, ERV is not interpretable.
  • Not used for diagnosis: ERV is never used alone to diagnose obstruction or restriction. It contributes to calculating FRC (ERV + RV) but the individual value has limited standalone clinical utility.
⚠ A low ERV in an obese patient with normal FEV₁/FVC and normal TLC is NOT restriction. It's a positional/mechanical effect of obesity on the chest wall. KCO is often elevated in this setting — another clue.

Obstructive Pseudorestriction

One of the most common misdiagnoses in PFT interpretation. In severe obstruction, massive air trapping elevates RV. This RV "steals" from the VC — as RV goes up, FVC comes down. The FEV₁/FVC may now be near-normal (both are reduced proportionally) and the FVC is low → looks like restriction.

FVC low + FEV₁/FVC normal → "Possible restriction"
↓
Get TLC. Is TLC low, normal, or HIGH?
↓
TLC normal or ↑: NOT restriction. This is obstructive pseudorestriction — air trapping reduced the FVC. RV is elevated. TLC is your arbiter.
TLC ↓: True restriction confirmed. Now you can grade severity by TLC % predicted.
💡 Also check SVC vs FVC gap — if SVC is significantly higher than FVC, the "low FVC" was from dynamic collapse during forced exhalation, not a small lung.

When FEV₁/FVC Lies

  • Ratio artificially normal in restriction: In pure parenchymal restriction (e.g., IPF), both FEV₁ and FVC fall in proportion → ratio preserved or even elevated. The ratio alone will not flag this — you must look at the absolute FVC and FEV₁ values against LLN.
  • Ratio artificially elevated in mixed disease: If obstruction and restriction coexist, the FVC may fall more than FEV₁ → ratio rises toward normal → obstruction is masked. Always look at the TLC to unmask the obstruction component.
  • Ratio near LLN in a tall young male: LLN for FEV₁/FVC in a healthy 25yo male can be as low as 0.68–0.70. Using a fixed cutoff of 0.70 will misclassify him as obstructed. Always use LLN from reference equations, not a fixed number.
  • Ratio artificially high in poor effort: If the patient terminates early, FVC is truncated → ratio rises → looks like restriction or normal when the patient is actually obstructed. Check exhalation duration on VTC.

DLCO Pitfalls

  • Anemia not corrected: Every 1 g/dL drop in Hb below reference reduces DLCO ~4–5%. Always correct for Hb. An uncorrected DLCO in a severely anemic patient may be reported as "moderately reduced" when it's actually normal for their Hb.
  • COHb from smoking: Carboxyhemoglobin occupies Hb binding sites, reducing CO uptake. Heavy smokers who smoked within 4–6 hours of testing will have falsely LOW DLCO. Ask about recent smoking.
  • Alveolar hemorrhage: Paradoxically ELEVATED DLCO — free Hb in alveoli binds CO avidly. A rising DLCO on serial testing in a patient with hemoptysis is a red flag, not reassurance.
  • Recent exercise: Pulmonary capillary recruitment from exercise can transiently elevate DLCO for 30–60 min. Patients should avoid exercise before testing.
  • Breath-hold time: Must be 10 ± 2 seconds. Too short → DLCO overestimated. Too long → CO equilibrates → DLCO underestimated. Check technical parameters before interpreting.

Race-Based Equations — The Active Controversy

  • Old GLI-2012: Separate equations for Caucasian, Black, NE Asian, SE Asian — Black reference values set ~12% lower for FVC/FEV₁
  • ATS 2023 statement: Recommends race-neutral (global) equations as the preferred default — race is a social construct, not a biological variable, and lower predicted values for Black patients likely encode socioeconomic and environmental disadvantage, not physiology
  • Practical consequence: A Black patient previously tested with race-specific equations may have "normal" results that become below LLN when race-neutral equations are applied — potentially unmasking real disease that was systematically hidden
  • Your lab policy: Confirm your PFT software default. If still using race-specific equations, flag this in your reports and consider dual reporting during the transition period
  • DLCO and volumes: Race-based equations exist for these too (GLI-2017, GLI-2021) — same controversy applies

Longitudinal PFT Interpretation

  • Meaningful decline in FVC (ILD monitoring): ≥10% relative decline OR ≥200 mL absolute over 12 months = clinically significant progression in IPF (per ATS/ERS IPF guidelines)
  • Meaningful decline in DLCO: ≥15% relative decline over 12 months = significant in ILD and vascular disease monitoring
  • Use same reference equations across visits: Switching from race-specific to race-neutral equations will change all values — do not interpret this as disease progression
  • Natural aging effect on FEV₁: FEV₁ declines ~25–30 mL/year in healthy non-smokers; ~40–50 mL/year in smokers. A "stable" absolute FEV₁ over years actually means the patient is declining relative to their predicted value.
  • Height changes in elderly: Height loss from vertebral compression changes the predicted values. Always remeasure height at each visit — do not carry forward a height from 10 years ago.
